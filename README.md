# Microneedle_detector
It's a microneedle patch needle detector. This small application is mostly made for the experiments. 
Goal: To detect the dissolution percentage of microneedle needles after being inserted into the an accelarated dissolving environment.
How to: First you need to make an imaging chamber. Full instructions: 
Reproducible spatial registration between pre- and post-dissolution images was a fundamental requirement of this method, since the image analysis software computed dissolution by direct pixel-area comparison and contained no correction algorithm for positional misalignment — any geometric difference between sessions would introduce systematic error into the dissolution percentage. To eliminate this source of variability, a custom imaging apparatus was designed and fabricated (Figure X). The device consisted of an open-top rectangular chamber with all interior surfaces coated matte black to eliminate ambient light reflections during microscopy. An angled platform fixed within the chamber served as the mount for the microneedle patch. To identify the optimal imaging inclination, five platform angles were evaluated — 45°, 50°, 55°, 60°, and 65° — with 60° selected on the basis of superior tip visibility and image contrast. A light microscope with 2× magnification was positioned above the open aperture of the chamber; all images were captured at a fixed lens-to-sample distance of 10 cm and full HD resolution (1920 × 1080 pixels). A custom-fabricated mechanical holder secured the chamber position to prevent displacement between sessions.
Four parameters were strictly standardized and held constant across both imaging sessions: platform inclination angle, chamber-to-lens position, lens-to-sample working distance, and method of patch fixation to the platform. Prior to each dissolution experiment, a small reference mark was applied to the microneedle patch surface using an indelible pen. This landmark confirmed that the identical region of the array was centered in the microscope field of view in both pre- and post-dissolution sessions, enabling precise spatial registration.
You first put patch into a gelatin film. before and after you should take photo of microneedle. When you first start application it ask you of the before image then it will ask you for after image. At both before and after images the application tries to automate the needle location and needle areas. it will be 70% likely correct too. But as long as you may face a chalenge of blurry images like i did. I suggest manually correcting the needle locations and Area of every needle. 
For manually correcting: for the needles location on patch green dots represents how application thinks of needle location. Clicking on green dots will cancel it. Clicking on blank area, likely a missed needle will make a green dots. For area correction a triangle is assumed of how needle will look like at screen. So there will be 3 dots. moving them at every needle angles will make a triangle. likely it's showing where the area is. After that application will try to calculate the dissolution percentage.
